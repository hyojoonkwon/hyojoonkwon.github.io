Finding My Daily Motto

1) How I Discovered BIM — and Why I Decided to Pursue It

Graduating with a degree in architecture, I was certain I would follow that path no matter what. I wanted real practice more than anything, and rather than spending time as a job-seeker I planned to gain experience steadily and move upward through the industry.

In practice, however, most of my time was spent on SketchUp visualisations and Lumion renders for clients, and on CAD drawings that were endlessly revised to satisfy legal constraints or client preferences — adding an extra bathroom to a multi-unit building to squeeze out a few more million won, for instance. The design itself barely mattered; what mattered was threading the needle of regulations and client whims, revision after revision.

The deeper frustration was that the 3D visualisation tools and the actual drawing deliverables couldn't be edited simultaneously and weren't even synchronised. I believed Revit could solve that problem, so after work I started attending Revit classes at a computer academy in Gangnam — funded through the national skills-training card — and that's how I came to learn about BIM (Building Information Modeling).

After completing two years of practice (and once my small-business savings bond matured), I decided that picking up BIM properly on the job would take too long. So I applied for a master's programme at UST (University of Science and Technology) under Dr. Moon Hyun-seok in the BIM cluster, where I could study and work on real research projects simultaneously as a student researcher.

2) What I Learned After Discovering BIM

Studying under Dr. Moon opened doors to an incredible volume of information and a wide network of people. Through hands-on implementation of research projects and automation pipelines, I saw that manual, non-automated workflows were the norm not just in architecture but across civil engineering and port development too. I took on the role of bridging those gaps — and in doing so, I began to believe I could actually build things through automation.

That said, there was a nagging frustration. The research agenda required chasing AI-related topics and producing polished-looking results using the latest technologies — essentially working two or three steps ahead of what practitioners actually needed. In real meetings with industry professionals, the consistent feedback was: 'It's impressive, but we're not ready to use it yet' or 'The implementation is meaningful, but adoption is still too difficult.' I kept wondering whether any of it was genuinely useful.

It was in that context that I asked Park Jin-hoon, principal of Flor Architecture — someone I'd met through a C# group training session — a question that ended up clarifying everything:

Me: What's the difference between the role of a researcher and a developer in R&D?

Park Jin-hoon: Well, as you've experienced — researchers using AI and the latest technologies need to be thinking and building two or three steps ahead of current practice. But a developer only needs to be half a step ahead.

That exchange made it clear: what I actually wanted to do was development — not research.

3) The Master's Journey — and the Decisions That Follow

Honestly, those two years of the master's programme (still ongoing as of 25.06.23) were chaos. The sheer volume of work, combined with the relentless drive to 'try harder' and 'be more committed,' slowly ate away at me. The final semester was genuinely hard to get through mentally. Things I thought would just pass turned out to still be holding me back — loose ends I should have tied up properly but didn't.

As the end approached, I started facing questions I'd been dodging all along: What can I actually do? What do I need to do to close that gap between research and practice? Writing this post is partly my way of forcing myself to confront them.

Here are the questions I'm sitting with now:

  • I'm nearly done with my master's in BIM. Where do I actually want to go from here?

  • Overseas employment is something I need to consider — but in the meantime I've been given an opportunity to build my career in Korea for a year. How do I make the most of it?

  • Thanks to putting in the effort during my master's (imperfect as it was), two offers have come in: one from Prof. Lee Jae-wook at Gachon University to work on a Samsung MEP factory production project; the other from Basis office, who asked whether I'd be interested in joining them to build internal automation processes and develop Dynamo logic — instead of going somewhere like Sangsnag-Jinhwa. How do I approach both to get the most out of them?

  • The reason I got into BIM in the first place: I loved architecture but lacked confidence in design. What I was good at was the software side. And one day I came across a Foster + Partners job posting for a BIM Coordinator — I couldn't solve the design problems, but I thought: what if I could build the BIM environment that helps those who do? I want to hold onto that feeling, and keep developing the capability to realise it.

4) Conclusion

There's still time before decisions need to be made — and there's still a lot left to resolve before I can even get there. I've been getting ahead of myself, lining up criteria for what comes next before I've properly closed out what's right in front of me. As Prof. Lee Jae-wook put it: a 'beautiful farewell' means finishing what you started — not perfectly, but with an actual ending.

Don't be too hard on yourself. Whatever you were doing in that moment — you did your best. It's okay if things weren't completed in time. Just make sure every problem gets seen through to a result. Love yourself. It's okay. It's all okay.

Love yourself and enjoy the ride.
